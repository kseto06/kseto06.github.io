Aegis

A Design for a Right-Hook Collision Detection and Prevention System

Kaden Seto, Kimmy Tran, Oscar Liang, Sarah Wang

Github Request for Proposal (RFP)

Summary:

For my Praxis II design project, my Praxis team (me, Kimmy, Oscar, and Sarah) teamed up to address the issue of right-hooking in the City of Toronto. Right-hooking is a common type of cyclist incident that occurs in Toronto and is one of the major causes of the many yearly cyclist injuries and deaths. Right-hooking occurs when a vehicle fails to check a passing cyclist in their blindspot while making a right turn, resulting in the vehicle colliding with the cyclist. As avid cyclists, my team and I are always worried about the safety of Toronto cyclists and we wanted to tackle this opportunity.

In this project, we combined engineering design concepts, machine intelligence, electrical engineering, and classical mechanics to develop an infrastructure-based cyclist detection camera system with light activation that aims to alert drivers of incoming cyclists in their blindspot. This project depended heavily on our knowledge and application of the FDCR model in order to create a high-quality, feasible solution that would fit the needs of the Toronto cycling community. We communicated heavily with the leader (Jun Nogami) of the Advocacy for Respect for Cyclists (ARC) group and had to constantly rescope and reframe our community, designs, and requirements in order to create a design that benefits cyclists and also can be easily implemented on top of current infrastructure in Toronto.

FINAL DESIGN: Aegisv2 (Camera + Arduino Lights)

We initially framed our opportunity through meeting our community (ARC) to discuss their lived experiences, challenges, and their needs and objectives. We learned that Nogami (leader of ARC) organizes ghost bike memorials to honour the lives of cyclists who have died in Toronto due to weak law enforcement and lack of driver awareness. He feels tremendous emotional loss towards the lives of cyclists taken in Toronto, saying, “I remember every single one and every single name”. Through this, we learnt the direct impact of ARC's organized ghost bike memorials and protests, and we were inspired to tackle this opportunity to help prevent future deaths of cyclists in Toronto, specifically in right-hooking incidents.
In our Request for Proposal (RFP), we did further research into right-hooking incidents, stakeholders, and reference designs to design a detection system that would be able to effectively detect and warn vehicles of incoming cyclists. Through research and rigorous classical mechanics calculations, we were able to design requirements and objectives that determined the required distance and time to be kept between the bike and the car in order to have both objects stop in time to prevent a right-hooking collision. Reference designs such as existing blind spot sensors and car horns helped us diverge potential detection and alertion systems for our final design. I was most involved in modeling the system and doing the physics calculations to define the requirements and objectives for our Requirements Framework, where I was able to apply the knowledge that I learnt in PHY180 Classical Mechanics to model real-life physics scenarios.

In the diverging stage, we diverged to three main designs using various diverging tools, most notably Lotus Blossom and Biomimicry. We developed Aegis (camera detection system), Sensio (Arduino flagpole system), and Ekko (car horn sound system) as ways to detect and alert vehicles of incoming cyclists. Our approach to these designs were very different, with each design touching on different aspects and types of engineering. I was mainly involved in developing Aegis, where I applied my knowledge of machine intelligence in computer vision to train a cyclist detection model using YOLOv11 with transfer learning, and also explored developing fast-inference super resolution GAN models (SRGAN) to help with image quality upscaling for detection. The model achieves high precision values (mAP75 ~ 95%) and was able to accurately and quickly detect cyclists for edge-detection (2.7ms inference times) during our validation tests.

Upon our final meeting with the community and getting our final piece of feedback from Nogami, we learnt that designs implementing small-scale infrastructure changes are most desirable because it is hard to force/convince drivers and cyclists to implement a solution on their own vehicles and bikes, and large-scale infrastructure changes take a long time to be implemented. With this in mind, we converged to an upgraded version of Aegis, which combines Aegis detection system, and Sensio's Arduino lights to create an alerting system attached beside traffic lights. The goal of the final design is to detect incoming cyclists in the bike lane and use flashing lights to indicate to drivers that there is a cyclist in their blindspot to prevent the potential right-hook collision.
